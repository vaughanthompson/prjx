Introduction. By Alle McGrath.


One of the things I enjoy most about my role is seeing the incredible work our people do every day. Sometimes it's the big achievements that get noticed, but more often it's the small things—helping a teammate, solving a problem, welcoming a new person to the team, sharing knowledge or going the extra mile for a customer—that really define who we are as a business.

As I looked through this month's newsletter, that was the common theme that stood out to me. Whether it's our Distribution Centre teams working together as one national network, Sean strengthening relationships with our OEM partners overseas, Andrew and Joe spending time with customers at Volvo Days, or celebrating Graham Walker's remarkable 39 years with TDX, every story is really about people. It's those relationships, the willingness to help each other, and the pride we take in our work that continue to make TDX a great place to be.

You'll also see a couple of stories this month that focus on safety. For me, safety is about doing the right thing because you genuinely care about the people you work alongside. It's looking out for your mates, speaking up when something doesn't seem right, and making sure everyone gets home safely at the end of the day. Every conversation, every improvement and every near miss reported is another opportunity to learn and make sure we are safe at work.

I hope you'll take a few minutes to read those articles and think about the part each of us plays in building the kind of culture we all want to be part of.

I hope you enjoy this month's edition.
Alle.

 

Safety Moment - Near misses are gifts.


That might sound like a strange thing to say, but every near miss gives us the opportunity to learn before someone gets hurt.

A near miss today could be tomorrow's serious incident. The only difference is that today, we have the chance to identify the problem, fix it and make sure nobody is injured.

If you've ever walked away from a job and thought, "That was close," those are exactly the moments we want to hear about. Every near miss tells us something about our workplace, our equipment, or the way we do our jobs.

Reporting a near miss isn't about getting someone into trouble—it's about helping everyone go home safely. We can't fix what we don't know about, and the best lesson is the one we learn before an incident occurs.

You may have heard of the Safety Triangle (sometimes called Heinrich's or Bird's Triangle). While the exact numbers have been debated over the years, the principle behind it remains incredibly relevant.


For every serious injury, there are many more minor incidents and an even greater number of near misses that happened beforehand. Those near misses are our early warning signs. They tell us where our systems, equipment, processes or behaviours have the potential to let us down before someone is seriously hurt.

That's why reporting near misses is so important. At TDX, we don't see them as paperwork or "nothing happened" events - we must see them as opportunities to learn. Every near miss gives us valuable information that helps us remove hazards, strengthen our controls and prevent someone else from being injured in the future.

The best time to learn from an incident is before someone gets hurt. That's why we encourage everyone to see something, say something, do something. Every near miss reported is a gift that helps us make TDX a safer place to work.

Building Safety Together

Over the past few months you've probably noticed there's been a lot happening across TDX in the safety space.

That's no accident.

As a business we've made the decision that we want to do more than just be compliant - we want to build a workplace where working safely is simply the way we do things.

That doesn't happen because the Safety Team writes another procedure or introduces another form. It happens when every one of us understands the critical risks in our work, speaks up when something doesn't look right, and looks out for each other. Its about doing the right thing when no-one is looking.

We've already made some fantastic progress.

Our Health & Safety Committees are now active right across the country, bringing together people from every part of the business to identify improvements, solve problems and share ideas. A huge thank you to everyone who gives their time to these committees. Your contribution is helping shape a safer TDX. I'd also like to recognise Nicky, who has done an outstanding job coordinating our committees nationally and keeping the momentum going.

We've also been refreshing our Risk Assurance Audits across our sites and the improvements have been impressive. Local teams have worked hard to close out actions, improve standards and make practical changes that genuinely reduce risk. Some sites have lifted their audit scores by almost 20%, which is a fantastic achievement.

The next step is making these reviews part of the rhythm of each site—not because someone asks, but because local teams see the value in regularly checking how they're doing, identifying opportunities and continuously improving.

You'll also hear us talking more about our critical risks. These are the things in our workplace that have the potential to cause serious harm if they're not managed well. Our focus is helping everyone understand these risks, the controls that keep us safe, and why they matter—not because a procedure says so, but because we all want to go home safely at the end of every day. This reflects the next stage of our safety journey: moving from following the rules to preventing harm before it happens by increasing leadership engagement, workforce ownership and learning. We are underway testing our Critical Risk Verification Tool in Christchurch at present and once we this fully complete, we will be bringing this to our other sites.

Safety isn't owned by the Safety Team or by the Leaders - it belongs to every one of us.

Together, we can continue building a workplace where safety isn't something we think about occasionally—it's simply the way we work.

As a comparison think of it like driving a vehicle on the road.


  1. It's a risky environment (speed, large mass, multiple parties)
  2. There are rules to be followed
  3. The situation or environment changes constantly
  4. Potential risks come at us from all directions
  5. We are in the drivers seat and in control of our vehicle

We navigate the roads daily and avoid the potential risks (most of the time).

We do this because we have learned and have an inbuilt awareness of potential danger based on learning and experience - our radar is generally always on.

An individuals lapse of concentration or a poor or rushed decision is when the accident occurs.

The workplace is very similar - it's our individual responsibility to have our safety situational awareness active at all times to protect ourselves and others around us.

Parts performance and distribution


Behind every part delivered to a customer is a Distribution Centre team working together to ensure the right product arrives at the right place, at the right time. Over the past year, TDX has been focused on strengthening this capability through investment in people, processes and collaboration across its national distribution network.


Championed by Ben Still, Stuart Hill and Dean MacKenzie, the programme has centred on three two key priorities: improving parts coverage, developing the team, and enhancing service levels for customers.

A key step has been the move away from relying heavily on temporary staff in the Distribution Centres. Instead, TDX has made a conscious decision to recruit experienced, full-time warehouse professionals. The approach was first implemented in Christchurch before being adopted at the Cambridge Distribution Centre following its relocation from Auckland.

Recruiting the right people has provided a strong foundation for team development, while also creating greater consistency across operations.

Another significant change has been the shift from working in individual silos to operating as one connected national team. Daily communication from Distribution Centres keeps everyone informed about workloads, priorities and upcoming activities, encouraging proactive planning rather than reactive problem-solving. Combined with improved systems, processes and up-to-date training, including Health and Safety, the result has been a stronger "One Team" culture across the business.

Cross-training has also become an important initiative, with team members now able to work across multiple warehouse functions. This flexibility ensures operations continue smoothly when colleagues are on leave or unavailable, helping maintain the high level of service customers expect.

These initiatives have contributed to improved service levels throughout the business, supported by more flexible transport arrangements, stronger inventory coverage and faster transit times. By continuing to invest in its people and ways of working, TDX is building a Distribution Centre network that keeps customers front of mind and is well equipped for the future.

 

Volvo Days 2026: Building Stronger Partnerships in Sweden


Every two years, Volvo Construction Equipment brings together customers, dealers and industry leaders from around the world for one of the biggest events on its calendar - Volvo Days. This year's event in Sweden was no exception, with around 4,000 attendees taking part over a five-week programme, welcoming approximately 800 visitors each week from Volvo markets across the globe.


Representing TDX were Andrew Falloon and Joe Gradwell, who hosted a group of ten customers on a memorable 10-day journey through Sweden. The group reflected the breadth of TDX's customer base, bringing together national accounts from both the North and South Islands, ranging from owner-operators through to large corporate customers.

For Andrew, the trip was about far more than seeing new machines.

"Volvo Days gives customers an incredible opportunity to experience the Volvo brand from the inside out," he says. "With so many new products launched over the past 12 to 18 months, it was the perfect time to showcase what's coming and where Volvo is heading."

The itinerary included exclusive visits to Volvo's articulated hauler factory, engine foundry and wheel loader manufacturing facility, giving customers a firsthand look at the engineering, technology and quality behind the machines they use every day.

There was also plenty of hands-on action at Volvo's Customer Experience Centre, where customers were able to test-drive the latest equipment and experience new technology for themselves.

"It really gave everyone an appreciation of what's possible," says Andrew. "Seeing how the machines are built and then getting behind the controls creates a much deeper understanding of the product."

One of the highlights of the visit was, of course, Volvo Days itself. The spectacular choreographed machine demonstrations showcased the latest equipment, while exhibition areas highlighted Volvo's expanding range of services, new technologies and specialised applications tailored to different industries and markets.

Sustainability was another major theme throughout the event.

Rather than focusing solely on electric or hydrogen-powered equipment, Volvo presented a balanced approach centred on delivering solutions that are both environmentally responsible and commercially viable.

"It was encouraging to see Volvo taking a practical approach," Andrew explains. "The message was about finding the right solution for each application, balancing performance, sustainability and the realities of how our customers operate."

The event also showcased Volvo's growing range of electric machines alongside innovations designed for specialised applications, demonstrating how equipment can be adapted to meet unique customer requirements.

While the technology was impressive, Andrew says the greatest value came from the conversations.

"One of the biggest benefits was simply spending time together," he says. "Over ten days you get to know people on a different level. It becomes less about business and more about understanding the person behind it."

Those conversations also created valuable opportunities for TDX to listen.

"We heard firsthand what customers value, what they're looking for from our service teams and where they see opportunities for improvement. That feedback is incredibly valuable and helps us continue to evolve how we support them."

The experience reflected many of the values that define TDX every day. From listening to customers and building authentic relationships, to embracing innovation and supporting practical sustainability, the trip demonstrated that successful partnerships are built on more than great products—they're built on trust, genuine engagement and a shared vision for the future.

For Andrew, Volvo Days reinforced why those relationships matter.

"The machines are always impressive, but it's the people and the conversations that make the biggest difference. That's what makes experiences like this so worthwhile."

Building Stronger Partnerships Across the Asia-Pacific


Keeping strong relationships with our OEM partners is an important part of ensuring TDX continues to deliver outstanding products and support to customers across New Zealand. Recently, Sean Cole represented TDX at the Perkins Asia-Pacific Dealer Conference in Hong Kong before hosting representatives from Rehlko Engines during a week-long visit to New Zealand.

The two events provided valuable opportunities to strengthen partnerships, share knowledge and showcase the work TDX is doing with customers and OEMs throughout the country.
Perkins Charts a Path for Growth

Held in Hong Kong in early June, the Perkins APAC Dealer Sales Conference was the first regional gathering since 2018, bringing together distributors from across the Asia-Pacific region.

"It was fantastic to reconnect with the Perkins network after several years," says Sean. "The conference gave us the opportunity to meet the new leadership team, reconnect with existing contacts and hear firsthand about Perkins' plans for the future."

Delegates were introduced to Richard Hemmings, Perkins' new Vice President of Sales and Marketing, who has been with the company for more than 20 years, along with leaders from across the APAC business.

A key theme throughout the conference was Perkins' ambitious growth strategy, built around three core pillars: engine sales, aftermarket support and service. The overarching goal is doubling revenue by 2030.

"The Asia-Pacific region has been identified as Perkins' strongest growth market across all three areas," Sean explains. "There was also a strong focus on supporting the distributor network, introducing new products such as the upcoming 5000 Series engines, and making it easier for distributors to succeed."

One significant announcement was the transition from the current Distributor Excellence Programme to a new 'Distributor of the Future' platform from the end of 2026. The new programme is designed to reduce the administrative burden on distributors while providing greater financial incentives.

For Sean, the opportunity to network with other Perkins distributors from around the region was just as valuable as the formal presentations.

Showcasing New Zealand Innovation with Rehlko

Shortly after returning from Hong Kong, Sean hosted a representative from Rehlko Engines for a week-long tour of customers and OEM partners across New Zealand, culminating with visits to Fieldays.

The itinerary highlighted the innovative work being undertaken by New Zealand manufacturers using Rehlko diesel and petrol engines.

One highlight was a visit to Cloudy Bay in Blenheim, where the team viewed a prototype autonomous vineyard vehicle powered by a Rehlko KDI3404TCR Stage V diesel engine. TDX is also supplying and installing DAFO fire suppression systems on each of the prototype machines and any machine moving forward.

The tour also included a visit to Robotics Plus in Tauranga to see another autonomous vineyard machine powered by the Rehlko KDI3404TCR Tier 4 Final engine. TDX already supplies the Rehlko KDI2504TCR engine for the company's smaller autonomous platform, demonstrating the growing partnership between the organisations.


Additional visits took the team from Auckland through to Hawke's Bay, meeting with a range of OEM customers and importers before spending time together at Fieldays.

"Fieldays was a fantastic opportunity to catch up with many of our partners in one place and showcase the range of applications where Rehlko engines are being used," says Sean.

The visit also reinforced the strength of the relationship between TDX and Rehlko.

"Rehlko was extremely positive about the support TDX provides across engine sales, parts, service and technical product support. It's great recognition of the work our teams do every day to support customers throughout New Zealand."

By representing TDX on the international stage and showcasing the innovative work of our local OEM partners, Sean helped strengthen key relationships that will continue to benefit our customers, our suppliers and the business well into the future.

Hero Staff Member - Graham Walker. Celebrating 39 Years of Outstanding Service


This month, our Hero a Staff Member recognition goes to someone whose knowledge, commitment and dedication have become part of the fabric of TDX. Congratulations to Graham Walker, who celebrated an incredible 39 years with the business, having joined Transmissions & Diesels Ltd on 22 June 1987.


To put that into perspective, 1987 was the year the All Blacks won the inaugural Rugby World Cup, Lotto launched in New Zealand and the country experienced the infamous Black Monday stock market crash.

According to Steve Wooff, Graham's journey with TDX began when he walked through the doors as a 15-year-old straight out of high school.

"My recruitment policy at the time was to bring in smart young people fresh out of school," Steve recalls. "They would start as delivery drivers before developing into parts specialists. Graham followed that path, as did a number of other long-serving TDX stars including Mark and Andrew Falloon, Ben Still and Matt Bale."

Nearly four decades later, that opportunity has certainly come to fruition for both Graham and TDX.

Steve describes Graham as the perfect combination of traditional parts expertise and modern technical capability.

"Graham is the perfect mix of old-school parts guy and tech savvy, which is an essential skill in today's world. His expertise across the many and varied product lines, both past and present, has made him the go-to person for supporting our parts teams across the country. His retained knowledge and product expertise are second to none."

One of the standout moments in Graham's career came when he represented TDX as the parts specialist in the prestigious Global Volvo Masters Competition. Working alongside two service technicians, the team was challenged to diagnose and repair complex faults that had been deliberately embedded in Volvo machines. Success required exceptional technical knowledge and an in-depth understanding of Volvo's parts systems.

The result? A global victory for the TDX team.


"The spare parts component of the competition required a deep understanding of Volvo's parts systems, and Graham certainly has that in spades," says Steve.

Today, Graham serves as National Parts Manager, a role built on nearly 40 years of accumulated knowledge, practical experience and unwavering commitment to customer service. His willingness to help, strong work ethic and hands-on approach have earned the respect of colleagues and customers alike, making him an invaluable resource across the entire business.

After 39 years, Graham continues to embody the values that have helped make TDX what it is today.


Thank you, Graham, for your remarkable contribution to the TDX story. Congratulations on this outstanding milestone and thank you for nearly four decades of giving it your all.
